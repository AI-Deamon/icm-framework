# The 400-Year History of Thinking Machines

**Source:** Skool: The Archive / Module 1 / 1.2 A 2,000-Year Overnight Success

## Core Idea

The question "can machines think?" did not start with Turing in 1950 — it started with Descartes in 1637. He argued that no matter how perfectly a machine imitated a human, you could always test it: can it use language creatively rather than mechanically rearranging signs? That's exactly the Turing test, 300 years early.

## Why It Matters

- Every AI debate we're having today (can LLMs really think? are they just stochastic parrots? do they have understanding?) is a 400-year-old argument with new technology. Knowing the history stops you from thinking you're having a new insight.
- Descartes drew the line at flexible language use — exactly what LLMs now blur. The goalposts have moved from "can it talk" to "is it conscious" to "does it have feelings" as the technology advances. The pattern: we keep redefining what counts as "real" thinking to exclude whatever machines can currently do.
- The Turing test passing in 2024 wasn't the end of the argument — it was just the latest round. The debate will shift to something else (maybe "can it suffer?" or "does it have goals of its own?").

## How I'll Apply It

When reading hot takes about AI being "actually conscious" or "definitely not conscious," check what definition of thinking the author is using. 9 times out of 10, they're moving goalposts that have been moving for 400 years.
